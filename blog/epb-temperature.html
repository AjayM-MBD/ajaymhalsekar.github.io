EPB motor winding temperature — i²R losses and thermal behavior

Overview: This article explains why the Electronic Parking Brake (EPB) motor winding temperature can exceed 100 °C, how i²R losses cause heating, how to model thermal behaviour, and practical diagnostics & mitigations.

1. Fundamentals — i²R losses and where heat appears

When current flows through a motor winding or coil, resistive losses occur. The instantaneous electrical power dissipated as heat is:

P(t) = I(t)^2 · R

where I(t) is instantaneous current and R is the winding resistance. For a time-varying current, the average loss over a period T is:

P_avg = (1/T) ∫_0^T I(t)^2 dt · R

2. Why temperature can exceed 100 °C

  • Continuous high current: If the EPB is holding torque or repeatedly actuated without sufficient cool-down, cumulative energy raises the winding temperature.
  • High resistance at contacts: Poor connectors or partial shorts increase local heating.
  • Mechanical binding or increased torque load: The motor draws more current to overcome friction or jam, increasing i²R losses.
  • Ambient and packaging: Enclosure with poor heat-sinking and high ambient reduces heat dissipation.

3. Simple thermal model (lumped parameter)

A commonly used first-order thermal model treats the winding as a lumped thermal mass with thermal capacitance C_th (J/K) and thermal resistance R_th (K/W) to ambient:

C_th · (dT/dt) = P_loss(t) - (T - T_ambient)/R_th

Where P_loss(t) = I(t)^2 · R. At steady-state (dT/dt = 0):

T_ss = T_ambient + P_avg · R_th

This formula shows why high average power and high thermal resistance lead to high steady-state temperature.

4. Example calculation

Assume:

  • Winding resistance R = 2 Ω
  • RMS current during hold I_rms = 3 A
  • P_avg = I_rms^2 · R = 9 · 2 = 18 W
  • Thermal resistance R_th = 3 K/W, ambient 40 °C

Then steady-state temperature:

T_ss = 40 + 18 * 3 = 94 °C

If current increases to 4 A, P_avg = 32 W → T_ss = 40 + 32*3 = 136 °C (exceeds 100 °C).

5. Where does heat concentrate?

Heat is generated in:

  • Winding copper (bulk resistive loss)
  • Local hotspots at poor solder joints or connector pins
  • Magnetic losses (eddy current & hysteresis) in iron if present (less in small EPB motors but not zero)

6. Diagnostics & measurements

  • Measure motor current during actuation and holding phases — use RMS measurement or log I(t).
  • Measure winding resistance at cold and after thermal stabilization; rising R with temperature is expected (R(T)=R0[1+α(T-T0)]).
  • Thermistor or temperature sensor mounted near the winding — for in-field protection and data logging.
  • CAN signals & ECU logs — monitor duty-cycle, torque requests and fault counters.

7. Mitigations

  1. Reduce average current: Use PWM hold methods with low duty cycle and enough refresh rate to reduce RMS current while preserving torque.
  2. Thermal protection: Implement overtemperature thresholds and automatic rest cycles or derating.
  3. Mechanical fixes: Reduce friction, ensure proper lubrication and mechanical free-play to avoid high torque draw.
  4. Improve heat dissipation: Better thermal path from winding to housing, use heat-conductive potting or heatsink features.
  5. Quality electrical connections: Proper crimping, soldering and connector selection to prevent localized overheating.

8. Simulink modelling (practical)

Create a simple Simulink model with three blocks:

  1. Current profile generator (step + noise or PWM averaged value)
  2. Loss calculator: I(t)^2 * R
  3. Thermal mass: Integrator for C_th and heat flow through R_th to ambient

This produces a temperature vs time plot that helps design safe duty cycles. (A sample .mdl/.slx file can be included in the repo — avoid sharing OEM-proprietary models.)

9. Practical test checklist

  • Log current and temperature during repeated actuations
  • Measure connector temperatures with thermal camera
  • Confirm R_th via steady-state test with known power
  • Test failure modes: jam, binding, shorted turns

10. Conclusion

Winding temperature depends on average i²R losses, thermal resistance to ambient, and duty cycle. Simple lumped-parameter thermal models give good first-order estimates and are useful to create thermal protection strategies in EPB ECUs.
